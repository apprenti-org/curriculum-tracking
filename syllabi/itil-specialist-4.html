Summative assessment strategy. A cumulative, certification-style multiple-choice assessment modeled on the ITIL 4 CDS / HVIT exam format (40 multiple-choice questions, 90-minute window, 70% pass mark) as a design reference — this is a course summative, not PeopleCert exam prep. Questions are weighted toward the two topics this course owns (monitoring/observability and HA/resiliency/fault tolerance) and toward the exam-critical key-term distinctions called out in each module (incident vs. problem vs. known error; change enablement vs. release vs. deployment; SLI vs. SLO vs. SLA vs. OLA). Scenario items require applying a practice to an operational situation rather than reciting a definition.

Integrated capstone scenario. As part of the summative (no added course hours — the capstone is delivered within the distributed assessment time), the learner works one cumulative scenario that spans all four modules. A production service shows rising latency; the learner: (1) uses observability (metrics/logs/traces, golden signals) to diagnose the degradation (Module 1); (2) triages and escalates the incident with the correct severity and escalation path (Module 2); (3) performs root cause analysis (Module 2); (4) assesses a proposed fix through change enablement — classifying the change and running an impact/risk assessment (Module 3); and (5) verifies that the fix keeps the service within its error budget against the relevant SLO (Module 4). Deliverable: an incident report + RCA write-up + change assessment + an updated runbook.

Formative assessment strategy. Each lesson carries a `quiz.md` sized at `target_questions = ceil(5 × hours)` per the workspace quiz-sizing rule (e.g., 8 questions for a 1.5h lesson, 5 for a 1.0h lesson) plus the hands-on activity pairs listed per lesson (labs, code-alongs, demos, exercises). Each module ends with a module review gate before the next module begins.

Learning Outcomes

  1. Distinguish observability from monitoring and explain when predefined checks are insufficient
  2. Describe what metrics measure and the operational questions they answer well (and poorly)
  3. Describe what logs capture and explain when structured logging and correlation IDs matter
  4. Explain how distributed traces localize latency and errors across services using spans and trace context
  5. Differentiate white-box from black-box telemetry and explain how cardinality and signal cost constrain what to instrument
  6. Explain the purpose of the ITIL monitoring and event management practice and how it operates a live service
  7. Classify events as informational, warning, or exception and distinguish an event from an alert and an incident
  8. Apply health checks and synthetic monitoring to verify a service from inside and outside
  9. Use the four golden signals — latency, traffic, errors, saturation — to assess service health
  10. Design actionable alerts and explain how poor alerting causes alert fatigue and on-call burnout
  11. Apply event correlation and noise-reduction techniques to reduce alert volume without losing signal
  12. Describe how AIOps uses machine learning for detection, correlation, and event grouping to reduce alert fatigue
  13. Explain the purpose of incident management and distinguish an incident from a problem and a known error
  14. Walk the incident lifecycle from detection through logging, categorization, prioritization, diagnosis, resolution, and closure
  15. Prioritize an incident using an impact-by-urgency matrix and map it to SLA-driven response targets
  16. Apply functional and hierarchical escalation and describe the role of the incident bridge in a major incident
  17. Distinguish problem management from incident management and contrast reactive with proactive problem management
  18. Apply structured root-cause analysis techniques (5 Whys, fishbone/Ishikawa, fault-tree) to investigate an incident from evidence and timeline
  19. Produce a known-error record that links root cause to incidents and documents a workaround
  20. Structure a runbook and explain the operational knowledge lifecycle that keeps it current and discoverable
  21. Conduct a blameless post-incident review that captures findings and tracked follow-up actions
  22. Author a runbook from a worked incident as a reusable restoration asset
  23. Distinguish change enablement from release and deployment management, and classify a change as standard, normal, or emergency
  24. Conduct an impact and risk assessment for a proposed production change, including a backout plan
  25. Place a change correctly against the change authority, the CAB/ECAB, the change schedule, and freeze windows
  26. Distinguish release management from deployment management and explain how deploying differs from releasing
  27. Compare rollout patterns (big-bang, phased, blue/green, canary) and select one for a given risk profile
  28. Plan a phased release with a rollback path, feature flags, and in-production validation
  29. Explain how operational controls (approvals, gates, audit trail) layer onto automated CI/CD delivery
  30. Apply separation of duties and change records to establish traceability for automated releases
  31. Use post-implementation review and governance to continuously improve the change flow
  32. Distinguish high availability, fault tolerance, and resiliency, and identify the redundancy, failover, clustering, and load-balancing patterns behind them
  33. Explain graceful degradation and self-healing patterns and where each applies
  34. Apply availability math (MTTR, MTBF, the "nines") and analyze a topology for single points of failure
  35. Distinguish SLI, SLO, SLA, and OLA and explain how they chain together
  36. Define meaningful SLIs and SLOs for a service and relate them to uptime targets and operational reporting
  37. Compute an error budget from an SLO and apply it to balance reliability against feature velocity
  38. Explain chaos engineering as hypothesis-driven failure injection to verify resilience
  39. Describe service continuity management and distinguish RTO from RPO
  40. Outline backup and disaster-recovery approaches and the role of DR testing

Course Outline

1 Monitoring, Observability & Event Management 4 hours
Lesson: 1: Observability Principles: Metrics, Logs, and Traces (1.5h)
  • The three pillars of observability (metrics, logs, distributed traces) and what each answers
  • Monitoring vs. observability — when predefined checks are insufficient
  • White-box vs. black-box telemetry; cardinality and signal cost
  • Activities: Code-along (instrument a service / read a telemetry dashboard), exercise
Lesson: 2: Monitoring & Event Management in Operation (1.5h)
  • The ITIL monitoring & event management practice applied to a running service
  • Event classification: informational, warning, exception
  • Health checks, synthetic monitoring, and golden signals (latency, traffic, errors, saturation)
  • Activities: Lab (configure monitoring against a sample service), exercise
Lesson: 3: Alerting, Signal Quality & an Introduction to AIOps (1h)
  • Designing actionable alerts; thresholds, alert fatigue, and on-call sustainability
  • Event correlation and noise reduction
  • AIOps intro: ML-assisted detection, correlation, and event grouping (e.g., Datadog intelligent monitoring / PagerDuty ML event correlation) to reduce alert fatigue
  • Activities: Demo (AIOps correlation walkthrough), exercise
2 Operational Incident & Problem Management 4 hours
Lesson: 4: Incident Handling, Severity & Escalation (1.5h)
  • The incident lifecycle: detect, log, categorize, prioritize, diagnose, resolve, close
  • Severity/priority matrices (impact × urgency) and SLA-driven response targets
  • Functional vs. hierarchical escalation and the incident bridge
  • Activities: Lab (triage and escalate a simulated incident), exercise
Lesson: 5: Problem Management & Root Cause Analysis (1.5h)
  • Reactive vs. proactive problem management
  • RCA techniques (5 Whys, fishbone/Ishikawa, fault-tree); evidence and timeline reconstruction
  • Known-error records, workarounds, and the link back to incidents
  • Activities: Lab (perform an RCA on a worked incident), exercise
Lesson: 6: Knowledge, Runbooks & Incident Response Documentation (1h)
  • Runbook structure and the operational knowledge lifecycle
  • Blameless post-incident reviews and capturing follow-up actions
  • Documentation-as-restoration-asset: keeping runbooks current and discoverable
  • Activities: Code-along (author a runbook from an incident), exercise
3 Change Enablement, Release & Deployment in Operations 4 hours
Lesson: 7: Change Enablement & Impact / Risk Assessment (1.5h)
  • Change types: standard (pre-authorized — e.g., a recurring, pre-approved patch), normal (assessed — e.g., a DB upgrade routed through the CAB), emergency (e.g., a 3am production-outage fix)
  • Impact and risk assessment; the change authority and the CAB/ECAB
  • Change schedules, freeze windows, and backout planning
  • Activities: Lab (assess and classify a proposed production change), exercise
Lesson: 8: Release & Deployment Management (1.5h)
  • Release packaging, phasing, and rollout patterns (big-bang, phased, blue/green, canary)
  • Deployment management vs. release management in practice
  • Rollback, feature flags, and validating a release in production
  • Activities: Code-along (plan a phased release with a rollback path), exercise
Lesson: 9: CI/CD-Adjacent Controls & Change/Release Governance (1h)
  • Operational controls layered on automated CI/CD delivery (approvals, gates, audit trail)
  • Separation of duties, change records, and traceability
  • Governance, post-implementation review, and continuous improvement of the change flow
  • Activities: Demo (governance/audit-trail walkthrough), exercise
4 Reliability Engineering — HA, Resiliency, SLOs & Error Budgets 4 hours
Lesson: 10: High Availability, Redundancy & Fault Tolerance (1.5h)
  • HA architectures: redundancy, failover, clustering, load balancing
  • Fault tolerance, graceful degradation, and self-healing patterns
  • Availability math: MTTR/MTBF, the "nines," and single points of failure
  • Activities: Lab (analyze a topology for SPOFs and add redundancy), exercise
Lesson: 11: SRE, SLIs/SLOs/SLAs/OLAs & Error Budgets (1.5h)
  • Site reliability engineering and the SLI → SLO → SLA → OLA chain
  • Error budgets: defining, spending, and using them to balance reliability vs. feature velocity
  • Meeting uptime targets and reporting against operational service levels
  • Scope boundary: this course covers operating to SLAs/SLOs (meeting targets, computing error budgets, escalating breaches); SLA negotiation/design is out of scope (Strategist tier / customer-engagement)
  • Activities: Lab (define SLIs/SLOs and compute an error budget), exercise
Lesson: 12: Chaos Engineering, Service Continuity & Disaster Recovery (1h)
  • Chaos engineering: hypothesis-driven failure injection to verify resilience
  • Service continuity management; RTO/RPO and continuity planning
  • Backup, disaster recovery, and DR testing
  • Activities: Demo (chaos experiment walkthrough), exercise
